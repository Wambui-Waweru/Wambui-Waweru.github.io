Iterative Design

In this project, I designed an interface for an emerging startup. This project is split into three parts: (1) sketching ideas of the interface (2) converging into an interactive, high fidelity prototype (3) conducting User Testing on this final prototype.

Problem

Chosen Startup: Prenda

1. Purpose of Startup: Prenda is a startup that focuses on providing an alternative to traditional schools such as microschools that can be personalized to every student’s style of learning. It is a hybrid of home school and traditional school with an emphasis on project-based learning. It provides teachers or Guides (as they refer to them) with all the necessary material and support to create the best environment for students to learn, grow and build lifelong skills. 2. Type of interface on which to design: Responsive website mainly directed towards teachers/guides for planning, organized and ordering material for their class

Design Choices

For the hi-fi prototype, my primary color is orange because it is bright, playful and fitting for a “micro school” website. The other accent colors are black and white that are used for the text. I have two separate home pages where one informs general users about what Prenda is while the other home page is the logged in version for the teacher/guide. Once a teacher/guide has logged in, I have hi-fi prototypes of screens for ordering school supplies, managing courses, viewing assignments and grade report of students in addition to email and calendar. Since ordering school supplies and checking email is a frequent task that the guides will do, I have made the pages accessible from any part of the website by having permanent clickable icons on the top left corner. From a guides perspective, the most important navigation bars are courses and assignments which are placed at the center of the navigation tabs. Moreover, since Prenda focuses on personalized learning, I have incorporated a page that allows guides to customize their course to fit each of their student’s way of learning.

Qualitative analysis of results [Errors (slips, lapses, or mistakes), and how satisfied users were when completing the tasks]: Completing the first task to find Arts & Crafts under assignments was a struggle for all the users because the first screen is the homepage where the guide is not yet logged in. They all tried to find the assignments in different tabs before trying to log in. Once logged in they completed the tasks pretty quickly. Another common point of confusion was when the users tried to customize courses. The button to do so (Edit chapter) was intuitive to them but when they received no response from the button, two of them interpreted that as a mistake. No user struggled to find the calendar, and all ranked the task as very easy.

Potential Design Changes

Increasing the accessibility of my site by increasing the contrast. Users have noted that while they like the color palette, the site may be too monochrome for those with visual impairments and some text was hard to read. Going forward, I would look more closely at web content accessibility guidelines to see what color combinations achieve better visual clarity. One user noted that the site gave an early “2010” feel, so to further make it a more modern site, I could reference popular modern websites for inspiration. Many of which go for a minimal, and flat appearance with simple and high contrast colors. Improve consistency in regard to visual appearance as the fonts being of different types and sizes make the site a bit jarring. Regarding overall layout, when watching users interact with my prototype, I noticed them getting a bit confused with navigation. In particular, it took a bit for users to figure out how to go from the landing page to the logged in page. I can make this process easier by increasing the size of the login buttons as well as making the overall buttons on the page in a more predictable layout. By predictability, users have noted that they would have liked the buttons on the page to not be so spread out as some go on pages only to get confused momentarily looking for the next button to click such as what happened with the “customize course” button, which was on the upper right hand corner of the page but could be moved to the top left corner instead because in general most of our users would be reading the screen from left to right.
